# Aurrigo International: Autonomous Airport Vehicle Production Deployments

## Company Overview

Aurrigo International plc (AIM: AURR) is a Coventry, UK-based company that designs, engineers, and manufactures autonomous ground support equipment (GSE) for airports. Founded by Professor David Keene, who has 39 years in the automotive industry (29 years ownership of Aurrigo), the company originated as RDM, an automotive supplier with electronics and software expertise. Aurrigo pivoted to aviation after International Airlines Group (IAG)'s Hangar 51 accelerator programme asked them to move a baggage container autonomously around Heathrow Terminal 5 — within one year they had an autonomous baggage dolly running at T5.

As of 2025, Aurrigo employs over 110 people at its Advanced Engineering Centre in Coventry, with technical offices in Canada (Ottawa), the US (Cincinnati), and Singapore. The company is publicly traded on AIM and has raised £14.1 million in 2025 to scale operations.

Keene introduced the concept of **iGSE** (intelligent Ground Support Equipment) — a paradigm shift where ground handlers transition from basic mechanized equipment toward autonomous, data-driven, sustainable systems.

## 2. Auto-DollyTug — Technical Specifications

### 2.1 Vehicle Concept

The Auto-DollyTug is a purpose-built autonomous electric vehicle that combines the functionality of a baggage tractor with a dolly, carrying a ULD onboard while simultaneously towing additional dollies. This means a combination of one Auto-DollyTug towing three trailers carries four ULDs — 30% more than a traditional tug setup of the same overall length.

Unlike legacy GSE manufacturers who retrofit diesel vehicles, Aurrigo designed the Auto-DollyTug from scratch as an autonomous electric vehicle. As Keene states: "We have never built a diesel-powered airport vehicle so we don't have to work around the compromises of a legacy GSE vehicle product."

### 2.2 Known Specifications

| Parameter | Auto-DollyTug | Auto-Cargo |
|-----------|---------------|------------|
| Powertrain | All-electric, lithium-ion, 88V | All-electric |
| Motors | Not disclosed | Twin 18.4kW |
| Onboard payload | 1 ULD (~1.5 tonnes) | 4,500 kg (standard pallet or 2 ULDs) |
| Towing capacity | Up to 4 conventional dollies or 2 cargo dollies | Up to 12,000 kg trailer |
| Total payload | Not disclosed | 16,500 kg |
| Max speed | Not publicly disclosed | 25 km/h |
| Runtime | Not disclosed | Up to 8 hours per charge |
| Steering | 4-wheel, 360-degree tank turn + sideways drive | 4-wheel steering |
| Loading | Bi-directional robotic arms, autonomous ULD load/unload | Precision automation |
| Weather | Rain algorithm up to 50mm/hr | Rain algorithm up to 50mm/hr |

**Auto-Shuttle (for reference):**
- 8-11 passengers (Mk2), 10-seat (Mk1)
- 30 bhp electric motor
- Up to 30 mph / 120+ mile range
- Ford E-Transit chassis (upfitted)
- Level 4 autonomy

### 2.3 Sensor Suite

Based on available public information:

- **LiDAR:** Multiple units — the Auto-Shuttle uses eight LiDAR units (mix of rotating and solid-state variants); Auto-DollyTug uses LiDAR for 360-degree obstacle detection. Custom housing protects sensors in wet weather.
- **Cameras:** 360-degree camera coverage. The Auto-Shuttle uses eight cameras. 3D cameras used for environment mapping (confirmed at Schiphol where the vehicle "mapped the platform environment using 3D cameras").
- **Ultrasonic sensors:** Positioned around the vehicle (confirmed on Auto-Shuttle).
- **Sensor fusion:** All sensor data is processed to create a 360-degree environmental view used for obstacle detection, path planning, and safe navigation.

### 2.4 Software Stack — Auto-Stack

Aurrigo's autonomous driving software (ADS) is called **Auto-Stack** and is developed entirely in-house. It is a proprietary system — there is no public confirmation of ROS usage.

Auto-Stack consists of software modules controlling:
- **Vehicle control:** Steering, braking, drive power management
- **Sensor integration:** Multiple sensor fusion and processing
- **Mapping:** Location mapping of the operational environment
- **Localization:** Localizing the vehicle to its map
- **Navigation:** Route planning and guidance
- **Communications:** External communication gateways
- **Cybersecurity:** Cyber resilience systems
- **Safety:** Vehicle safety system monitoring and intervention

The system uses five spatial zones around the vehicle for environmental perception, with zone-specific filtering (confirmed in the rain algorithm context where "filtering of the rain is split into five spatial zones around the vehicle").

### 2.5 Navigation Approach

Based on observed behavior at Schiphol, the Auto-DollyTug uses a map-learn-navigate approach:
1. The vehicle first maps the operational environment using 3D cameras
2. It learns to navigate the mapped environment independently
3. During operation, it localizes itself to the pre-built map and follows planned routes

The system processes LiDAR and camera data to determine "if it's safe, whether there's something coming" and "how to react" if obstacles enter "the safety bubble" (Tenille Houston, VP).

### 2.6 Unique Mechanical Features

- **Patented sideways drive system:** Enables direct lateral movement — the vehicle can slide sideways into tight spaces in baggage halls and on the apron. This is critical for confined-space operations around aircraft stands and in busy baggage halls.
- **360-degree tank turn:** The vehicle can rotate within its own footprint.
- **Bi-directional robotic arms:** Auto-loading and release management system enables fully autonomous ULD pickup and delivery without human intervention.

### 2.7 Generations

The Auto-DollyTug is now in its 3rd generation (as of January 2024, tested at Changi), with each generation representing significant redesigns rather than incremental improvements. Earlier generations were tested at Changi from February 2022 onward, providing operational experience that informed the current design.

- Fleet management interface connecting to flight schedules, lateral assignments, ULD numbers, priorities, and baggage handling system information

## 4. Operational Reality

### 4.1 Human Supervision Requirements

**Current state:** All deployments currently require a safety operator onboard. "For the purpose of testing and in line with current legislation, a safety operator will be onboard at all times" (Aurrigo). A second staff member may also be present to collect data for regulatory bodies.

**Path to full autonomy:** Aurrigo is actively working on removing the safety operator through the "Runway to Autonomy: No User in Charge (NUIC)" feasibility study (£99,425, 9 months), conducted in collaboration with IAG. This project aims to define:
- System failure protocols
- Certification pathways
- Cybersecurity standards
- Human-machine interaction models

Additionally, the **SAMS project** is developing a cyber-secure remote supervision protocol for autonomous vehicles — an important step toward commercial deployment where a remote operator oversees multiple vehicles rather than having one person per vehicle.

### 4.2 Conditions Handled

- **Wet weather:** Proprietary rain algorithm distinguishes raindrops from obstacles at up to 50mm/hr precipitation. Custom LiDAR sensor housing protects against water ingress. The algorithm splits filtering into five spatial zones around the vehicle with zone-specific height settings and near-field low-profile detection.
- **Hot/humid climates:** Validated at Changi Airport, Singapore.
- **Snow:** Tested in Ottawa, Canada.
- **Mixed traffic:** Demonstrated at Stuttgart operating alongside manual vehicles and pedestrians, yielding right-of-way appropriately.

### 4.3 Hours Operated / Distance Data

Aurrigo does not publicly disclose cumulative operating hours or kilometers driven. The longest-running deployment at Changi has been in testing since February 2022 (over 3 years of airside trial experience). The company states it has "ten of its autonomous vehicles now operating across the world" (as of late 2024).

### 4.4 Operational Maturity Assessment

All current deployments remain in pilot/trial phase. No deployment has reached fully unattended commercial operations. The East Midlands Airport ground handling licence (under CAA framework) represents the most advanced regulatory approval, but the licence is time-limited to June 2026 and "no commercial contracts are in place with airlines or cargo operators at EMA."

## 7. Safety Approach

### 7.1 Vehicle-Level Safety

- **360-degree obstacle detection:** LiDAR and camera sensor fusion creates a continuous environmental awareness bubble
- **"Safety bubble" concept:** The system determines if objects are entering the safety perimeter and how to react
- **Reliable detection of static and dynamic obstacles:** Demonstrated at Stuttgart with mixed traffic
- **Automatic emergency braking:** Vehicle can brake in time and maintain sufficient distances
- **Five spatial perception zones:** Environmental perception is divided into five zones around the vehicle, each with specific filtering and detection parameters

### 7.2 Regulatory Framework

- **UK:** Licensed under Airports (Ground Handling) Regulations 1997, in compliance with CAA framework under Civil Aviation Act 2012 (East Midlands Airport)
- **Germany:** Received backing from Federal Ministry of Digital and Transport; funded under DTAC through BMDV
- **Singapore:** Multi-year testing programme at Changi with airport authority approval
- **Netherlands:** Testing under Royal Schiphol Group oversight

There is no single global standard for autonomous airside vehicles. Aurrigo navigates airport-by-airport regulatory approval rather than a unified certification.

### 7.3 Standards and Certification

Aurrigo has not publicly cited specific safety standards (e.g., ISO 26262, IEC 61508, UL 4600) they certify against, though the NUIC feasibility study with IAG is explicitly developing certification pathways. The company operates within each airport's established safety and operational governance frameworks.

### 7.4 Transport Safety System (TSS)

A separate safety product (non-autonomous): electronic sensors on dollies communicate wirelessly to control units in tow tugs. If cargo is not locked down correctly, warnings appear on a head-up display. If anything falls from the dolly, the system detects weight change and immobilizes the tug. The tug is also immobilized if dolly uncoupling is detected. TSS was launched at Heathrow in 2019/2020 and can be retrofitted to existing fleets.

### 7.5 Safety Advantages of Autonomy

Aurrigo VP Tenille Houston: "One of the number one causes of accidents is fatigue and distraction." The autonomous system "doesn't get tired," remains "always focused," and "any deviation is recorded" — providing safety, reliability, and accountability.

### 7.6 Incident Record

No public reports of incidents, accidents, or near-misses involving Aurrigo autonomous vehicles have been found. The airport airside environment is more controlled than public roads, with restricted access, lower speeds, and established traffic protocols.

## 8. Challenges Encountered

### 8.1 Weather — The Rain Problem

The most publicly discussed technical challenge. Heavy rain causes LiDAR beam scattering and absorption by raindrops, leading to distorted signals and compromised environmental perception. Aurrigo's solution:
- **Proprietary rainfall algorithm:** Distinguishes rain from objects at up to 50mm/hr precipitation
- **Five spatial zone filtering:** Rain filtering is split across five zones around the vehicle. Differences in scan properties between filtered and unfiltered scans required special handling of low-lying obstacles and reflective surfaces
- **Hardware modifications:** New custom housing to better protect LiDAR sensors
- Successfully tested in both scheduled simulated rain and opportunistic heavy rainfall, verified by rain meter readings

### 8.2 Complex Airport Environments

Airports present unique challenges not found in road autonomy:
- Extremely congested spaces (baggage halls, aircraft stands)
- Mixed traffic with manual vehicles, GSE, and pedestrians
- Precise alignment requirements to loading equipment (high loaders, JCPL)
- Time-critical operations linked to flight schedules
- Multiple stakeholders and procedural requirements per airport

### 8.3 Regulatory Fragmentation

No unified global standard for autonomous airside vehicles exists. Each airport, national aviation authority, and country requires separate regulatory engagement. The NUIC feasibility study with IAG is explicitly working to define standardized certification pathways.

### 8.4 Scaling from Pilot to Production

As of early 2026, no Aurrigo deployment has reached fully unattended commercial-scale operations. The gap between successful pilot demonstrations and 24/7 commercial deployment remains significant. At East Midlands Airport: "no commercial contracts are in place with airlines or cargo operators at EMA."

### 8.5 Financial/Commercial Challenges

- FY2025 revenue declined to ~£8m from £8.9m in FY2024
- Autonomous division revenue was £2.5m in 2025 vs £2.9m in 2024 (though 2024 saw 433% growth)
- Tariff-related disruption affected UK OEM automotive customers in Q2 2025
- Extended delivery milestones for autonomous division programs pushed into FY26
- The company operates at an adjusted EBITDA loss (£1.6m in FY2024, improved from £3.2m prior year)
- Volatile macroeconomic environment impacts customer decision-making

### 8.6 Staffing and Labor

The technology is positioned to address airport labor shortages (a key driver cited by CVG, Swissport, and others), but Aurrigo itself needs to scale from 110 employees to support multiple simultaneous airport deployments globally.

## 9. Technology Roadmap

### 9.1 Near-Term: Remove the Safety Operator

The most critical near-term goal is achieving "No User in Charge" (NUIC) operation. The IAG-partnered feasibility study is defining the path. Parallel development of remote supervision (SAMS project) enables one remote operator to oversee multiple vehicles.

### 9.2 AI and Machine Learning

**Aston University KTP Partnership (announced February 2026):**
Funded by Innovate UK, this Knowledge Transfer Partnership is developing AI for fleet intelligence:
- Machine learning for intelligent agent systems
- Multi-agent systems for decentralized coordination
- Dynamic task allocation based on vehicle location, workload, and battery level
- Inter-fleet communication (vehicles from different ground handlers cooperating)
- Real-time adaptive planning for unexpected changes (flight delays, gate reassignments)
- Scalable task management as fleets grow

Academic team: Dr. Farzaneh Farhadi (ACAIRA — Aston Centre for AI Research and Application), Dr. Maria Chli, Professor Maia Angelova.

### 9.3 Virtual Reality and Simulation

**VABO Project (£98,642, 12 months):** Integrating immersive VR into baggage handling simulations using the Unity game engine. Enables real-time interaction, data visualization, and instant performance feedback for staff training within detailed 3D airport environments.

### 9.4 Battery and Platform Evolution

The £6.28m Ultra Global contract involves replacing lead-acid battery architecture with lithium-ion and updating HVAC, mechanical components, and integrating latest sensor technologies with advanced electrical control units.

### 9.5 Global Hub Expansion

Four principal hubs planned:
- **North America:** Cincinnati (established)
- **Southeast Asia:** Singapore and Malaysia
- **Middle East:** Dubai
- **Europe:** Amsterdam

Medium-term consideration of offshore manufacturing in South Asia and Southeast Asia.

### 9.6 Vision Statements

Professor Benjamin Bierwirth (Frankfurt University): "I expect these vehicles to be in continuous operational use at major airports within the next five years" (stated at Stuttgart, ~2024).

The company has not publicly discussed world models, foundation models, transformer architectures, or camera-primary perception approaches. Their technology stack appears to remain LiDAR-primary with camera support, using classical mapping/localization/navigation with emerging ML for fleet coordination rather than end-to-end learned driving.

## 11. Auto-Sim Simulation Platform

### 11.1 What It Is

Auto-Sim is a proprietary, cloud-based 3D digital twin simulation platform for airport airside operations. It models the complete airside environment to "visualize and optimize today's operations" and plan future autonomous deployments.

### 11.2 How It Works

**Inputs:**
- Airport flight schedules (primary driver)
- Baggage and cargo data models
- Airside road network mapping (indoor and outdoor)
- Vehicle fleet configurations (configurable via database and web GUI)
- Service procedures and turnaround requirements

**Simulation engine:**
- Driven by the airport's flight schedule
- Derives service "missions" required for every arrival and departure
- Optimizes all vehicle movements required for flight servicing and turnaround
- Simulates corresponding vehicle actions in detail

**Outputs:**
- Complete visibility of every event that occurs
- Data filtered into targeted metrics measuring KPIs
- Fleet sizing and routing optimization
- Business case modeling for autonomous vehicle deployment

### 11.3 Technical Architecture

- Cloud-based service
- Database and web GUI for configuration
- Simulation progress viewable via web browser from anywhere in the world
- 3D visualization (with planned VR integration via Unity — VABO project)

### 11.4 What It Simulates

- Flight schedule-driven vehicle movements across the entire airside
- Service missions for every aircraft arrival and departure
- Baggage and cargo operations workflows
- Representative road network (both indoor baggage halls and outdoor apron/taxiway areas)
- Fleet behaviors including routing, scheduling, charging, and multi-vehicle coordination
- Ground handling turnaround sequences

### 11.5 How It's Used for Validation

Auto-Sim serves three validation purposes:
1. **Pre-deployment business case:** Airports and ground handlers model the economic and operational case for adopting autonomous vehicles before committing to physical trials
2. **Operational optimization:** Identify automation opportunities, optimal fleet sizes, and routing in a virtual environment before real-world testing
3. **Phase 1 of deployment:** At Zurich (Swissport), Auto-Sim is deployed first to model the airside before Auto-DollyTug physical trials begin

### 11.6 Deployments

- **Zurich Airport:** Phase 1 of Swissport partnership
- **Amsterdam Schiphol:** Testing and validation underway (through Aviation Solutions B.V.)
- **Approved for marketing** across Aviation Solutions' network of 60+ airports

### 11.7 Relationship to Other Products

Auto-Sim is designed as the entry point for airport customers — model the business case in simulation, then deploy Auto-DollyTug/Auto-Cargo physically. Combined with Auto-Connect (fleet management) and Auto-Stack (vehicle autonomy), these four software platforms form Aurrigo's complete autonomous airside ecosystem.

## 13. Assessment: Gaps and Open Questions

1. **Detailed sensor specifications:** Exact LiDAR models (Velodyne? Ouster? Hesai?), camera resolution, compute platform (NVIDIA? custom?), GNSS/RTK setup
2. **Software stack architecture:** Whether Auto-Stack uses ROS underneath, what frameworks are used for perception/planning, whether any ML models are used in the perception pipeline
3. **Operational metrics:** Total hours operated, kilometers driven, ULDs moved, interventions per hour, mean time between disengagements
4. **Speed and range for Auto-DollyTug:** These fundamental specs are not publicly disclosed
5. **Safety case documentation:** Actual safety case methodology, hazard analysis approach, risk assessment framework
6. **Localization approach:** Whether they use GNSS/RTK, visual SLAM, LiDAR SLAM, or some combination for positioning
7. **Path to commercial viability:** When/whether the autonomous division will become profitable given current loss-making trajectory
8. **World models / learned perception:** No evidence of foundation model or end-to-end learning approaches; appears to be classical robotics stack
